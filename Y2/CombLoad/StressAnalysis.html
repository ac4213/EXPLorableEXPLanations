Introduction to Mechanical Stresses

Mechanical stress is a fundamental concept in engineering, representing the internal resistance of a body to external forces. Stress is defined as the force per unit area:

\[\sigma = \frac{F}{A}\]

where \(\sigma\) is stress, \(F\) is force, and \(A\) is the cross-sectional area.

Different types of loading produce different stress patterns within a material. Understanding these stresses is crucial for designing safe and efficient structures and mechanical components.

Key Types of Stress:

  • Normal stress (\(\sigma\)): Acts perpendicular to the cross-section (tension/compression)
  • Shear stress (\(\tau\)): Acts parallel to the cross-section

In this interactive lecture, we'll explore four fundamental loading scenarios and the stresses they produce.

Axial Stresses

Axial stress occurs when a force acts along the longitudinal axis of a member, creating tension or compression.

\[\sigma_{axial} = \frac{P}{A}\]

where \(P\) is the axial load and \(A\) is the cross-sectional area.

Axial stress is uniform across the cross-section for prismatic members, meaning it has the same value at every point in the cross-section.

Engineering Insight: Material behavior under axial loading follows Hooke's Law for small deformations, where strain is proportional to stress: \(\sigma = E \cdot \varepsilon\), with \(E\) being Young's modulus.

Practice Problem

A steel rod with a diameter of 25 mm is subjected to a tensile force of 20 kN. Calculate the axial stress in the rod.

Show Solution

Given:

  • Diameter, d = 25 mm = 0.025 m
  • Tensile force, P = 20 kN = 20,000 N

Step 1: Calculate the cross-sectional area

\[A = \pi \cdot \left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2 = \pi \cdot \left(\frac{0.025}{2}\right)^2 = 4.91 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2\]

Step 2: Calculate the axial stress

\[\sigma_{axial} = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{20,000}{4.91 \times 10^{-4}} = 40.7 \times 10^6 \text{ Pa} = 40.7 \text{ MPa}\]

Shear Stresses

Shear stress develops when forces act parallel to a cross-section, causing layers of the material to slide relative to each other.

\[\tau = \frac{V}{A}\]

where \(\tau\) is the average shear stress, \(V\) is the shear force, and \(A\) is the area resisting the shear.

In reality, shear stress is not uniform across most cross-sections. For rectangular sections, the maximum shear stress occurs at the neutral axis and is:

\[\tau_{max} = \frac{3V}{2A}\]

Engineering Insight: In beams, shear stress varies parabolically across the height of rectangular sections, with maximum value at the neutral axis. This is why I-beams have thick webs at the neutral axis to resist shear forces efficiently.

Practice Problem

A wooden beam with rectangular cross-section (width = 100 mm, height = 200 mm) is subjected to a shear force of 15 kN. Calculate the maximum shear stress in the beam.

Show Solution

Given:

  • Width, b = 100 mm = 0.1 m
  • Height, h = 200 mm = 0.2 m
  • Shear force, V = 15 kN = 15,000 N

Step 1: Calculate the cross-sectional area

\[A = b \cdot h = 0.1 \cdot 0.2 = 0.02 \text{ m}^2\]

Step 2: Calculate the maximum shear stress using the formula for rectangular sections

\[\tau_{max} = \frac{3V}{2A} = \frac{3 \cdot 15,000}{2 \cdot 0.02} = \frac{45,000}{0.04} = 1.125 \times 10^6 \text{ Pa} = 1.125 \text{ MPa}\]

Bending Stresses

Bending causes normal stresses that vary linearly across the height of a beam. These stresses are tensile on one side and compressive on the other, with zero stress at the neutral axis.

\[\sigma_{bending} = \frac{My}{I}\]

where \(M\) is the bending moment, \(y\) is the distance from the neutral axis, and \(I\) is the moment of inertia of the cross-section.

The maximum bending stress occurs at the furthest distance from the neutral axis:

\[\sigma_{max} = \frac{Mc}{I} = \frac{M}{Z}\]

where \(c\) is the maximum distance from the neutral axis, and \(Z = I/c\) is the section modulus.

Engineering Insight: I-beams are efficient for resisting bending because they concentrate material far from the neutral axis, maximizing the moment of inertia (I) while minimizing weight.

Practice Problem

A steel beam with a rectangular cross-section (width = 80 mm, height = 150 mm) is subjected to a bending moment of 10 kN·m. Calculate the maximum bending stress in the beam.

Show Solution

Given:

  • Width, b = 80 mm = 0.08 m
  • Height, h = 150 mm = 0.15 m
  • Bending moment, M = 10 kN·m = 10,000 N·m

Step 1: Calculate the moment of inertia for a rectangular section

\[I = \frac{bh^3}{12} = \frac{0.08 \cdot (0.15)^3}{12} = 2.25 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^4\]

Step 2: Find the maximum distance from the neutral axis

\[c = \frac{h}{2} = \frac{0.15}{2} = 0.075 \text{ m}\]

Step 3: Calculate the maximum bending stress

\[\sigma_{max} = \frac{Mc}{I} = \frac{10,000 \cdot 0.075}{2.25 \times 10^{-5}} = 33.33 \times 10^6 \text{ Pa} = 33.33 \text{ MPa}\]

Torsional Stresses

Torsion creates shear stresses that act perpendicular to the radius of the cross-section. For circular shafts, these stresses vary linearly with the radius.

\[\tau = \frac{Tr}{J}\]

where \(T\) is the applied torque, \(r\) is the distance from the center, and \(J\) is the polar moment of inertia.

The maximum torsional stress occurs at the outer surface of the shaft:

\[\tau_{max} = \frac{TR}{J} = \frac{T}{Z_p}\]

where \(R\) is the outer radius, and \(Z_p = J/R\) is the polar section modulus.

Engineering Insight: Hollow shafts are more efficient for resisting torsion than solid shafts of the same weight. This is why drive shafts in vehicles are typically hollow tubes.

Practice Problem

A solid circular shaft with a diameter of 50 mm is subjected to a torque of 2 kN·m. Calculate the maximum shear stress in the shaft.

Show Solution

Given:

  • Diameter, d = 50 mm = 0.05 m
  • Torque, T = 2 kN·m = 2,000 N·m

Step 1: Calculate the polar moment of inertia for a solid circular shaft

\[J = \frac{\pi d^4}{32} = \frac{\pi \cdot (0.05)^4}{32} = 6.14 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}^4\]

Step 2: Find the outer radius

\[R = \frac{d}{2} = \frac{0.05}{2} = 0.025 \text{ m}\]

Step 3: Calculate the maximum shear stress

\[\tau_{max} = \frac{TR}{J} = \frac{2,000 \cdot 0.025}{6.14 \times 10^{-7}} = 81.43 \times 10^6 \text{ Pa} = 81.43 \text{ MPa}\]

Combined Stresses

In real-world applications, structural elements often experience multiple types of stresses simultaneously. For example, a shaft might undergo both bending and torsion.

To analyze combined stresses, we use the principal stress theory and failure criteria like von Mises stress:

\[\sigma_1, \sigma_2 = \frac{\sigma_x + \sigma_y}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x - \sigma_y}{2}\right)^2 + \tau_{xy}^2}\]

von Mises stress: \[\sigma_{vm} = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 - \sigma_1\sigma_2 + \sigma_2^2}\]

Engineering Insight: When designing components subject to combined loading, engineers typically use the von Mises stress criterion to predict failure. This approach considers that materials typically fail due to shear strain energy rather than just maximum normal stress.

Practice Problem

A point in a loaded structural member is subjected to normal stresses of σx = 80 MPa and σy = 20 MPa, and a shear stress of τxy = 40 MPa. Calculate the principal stresses and the von Mises stress.

Show Solution

Given:

  • σx = 80 MPa
  • σy = 20 MPa
  • τxy = 40 MPa

Step 1: Calculate the principal stresses

\[\sigma_{avg} = \frac{\sigma_x + \sigma_y}{2} = \frac{80 + 20}{2} = 50 \text{ MPa}\]

\[R = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x - \sigma_y}{2}\right)^2 + \tau_{xy}^2} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{80 - 20}{2}\right)^2 + (40)^2} = \sqrt{900 + 1600} = \sqrt{2500} = 50 \text{ MPa}\]

\[\sigma_1 = \sigma_{avg} + R = 50 + 50 = 100 \text{ MPa}\]

\[\sigma_2 = \sigma_{avg} - R = 50 - 50 = 0 \text{ MPa}\]

Step 2: Calculate the von Mises stress

\[\sigma_{vm} = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 - \sigma_1\sigma_2 + \sigma_2^2} = \sqrt{(100)^2 - (100)(0) + (0)^2} = 100 \text{ MPa}\]

Conclusion

Understanding the different types of stresses and how they develop under various loading conditions is fundamental to mechanical and structural engineering. The key insights to remember are:

  1. Axial stress is uniform across the cross-section and directly related to the applied load and cross-sectional area
  2. Shear stress distribution depends on the cross-section geometry, with maximum values typically at the neutral axis for beams
  3. Bending stress varies linearly with distance from the neutral axis, with maximum values at the extreme fibers
  4. Torsional stress in circular shafts varies linearly with radius, with maximum values at the outer surface
  5. Real components often experience combined loading, requiring analysis of principal stresses and application of failure theories

These principles form the foundation for structural analysis and the design of safe, efficient mechanical components and structures.

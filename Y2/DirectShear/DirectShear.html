Understanding Shear Stress in Beams

Normal Stress vs. Shear Stress

When a beam is subjected to loading, two primary types of stress develop within the material:

Normal Stress (σ): Acts perpendicular to the cross-section

  • Caused by bending moments (M)
  • Results in tension and compression
  • Calculated using: \(\sigma = \frac{My}{I}\)

Shear Stress (τ): Acts parallel to the cross-section

  • Caused by shear forces (V)
  • Results in sliding deformation
  • Calculated using Jourawski's formula

Internal Shear Force and Shear Stress

When a beam carries transverse loads, internal shear forces develop at any cross-section to maintain equilibrium. These shear forces create shear stresses that vary across the height of the section.

The relationship between shear force and shear stress is not uniform across the section. Unlike normal stress from bending, shear stress follows a parabolic distribution in rectangular sections and more complex patterns in T-sections and I-sections.

Jourawski's Formula (Shear Stress Formula)

The Fundamental Equation

The shear stress at any point in a cross-section is given by:

\[\tau = \frac{VQ}{Ib}\]

Where:

  • \(\tau\) = shear stress at the point of interest
  • \(V\) = internal shear force at the section
  • \(Q\) = first moment of area above (or below) the point
  • \(I\) = second moment of area of the entire section
  • \(b\) = width of the section at the point of interest

Understanding Q - The First Moment of Area

Q represents the first moment of the shaded area (above or below the point of interest) about the neutral axis:

\[Q = A' \cdot \bar{y}'\]

Where:

  • \(A'\) = area of the shaded region
  • \(\bar{y}'\) = distance from the neutral axis to the centroid of the shaded area

Derivation of Jourawski's Formula

The derivation begins with considering the equilibrium of a differential element of the beam:

Step 1: Consider a beam element of length dx

At position x: Moment = M, Normal stress = \(\sigma_1 = \frac{My}{I}\)

At position x + dx: Moment = M + dM, Normal stress = \(\sigma_2 = \frac{(M + dM)y}{I}\)

Step 2: Force equilibrium on the shaded area

The difference in normal forces creates a horizontal force:

\[\Delta F = \int_{A'} (\sigma_2 - \sigma_1) dA = \int_{A'} \frac{dM \cdot y}{I} dA = \frac{dM}{I} \int_{A'} y \, dA = \frac{dM \cdot Q}{I}\]

Step 3: Relate to shear stress

This force must be balanced by shear stress on the horizontal plane:

\[\tau \cdot b \cdot dx = \frac{dM \cdot Q}{I}\]

Step 4: Apply the relationship dM/dx = V

From beam theory, we know that \(\frac{dM}{dx} = V\), therefore:

\[\tau = \frac{VQ}{Ib}\]

Shear Stress Distribution Patterns

Key Observations:

  • Shear stress is zero at the extreme fibres (top and bottom) due to \(Q=0\)
  • Maximum shear stress typically occurs at the neutral axis for symmetric sections
  • For T- and I-sections, the sudden change in thickness results in a jump of stress at the junction of flange and web

Practical Applications

Design Considerations

Understanding shear stress distribution is crucial for:

  • Web Design: Determining the required thickness of beam webs
  • Connection Design: Calculating shear flow for fastener spacing
  • Composite Beams: Analysing interface shear between materials
  • Failure Prevention: Avoiding shear failure in short, deep beams

Common Failure Modes

Shear stress can cause several types of failure:

  • Web Buckling: Thin webs may buckle under high shear stress
  • Diagonal Tension: Creates principal stresses at 45° to the beam axis
  • Horizontal Shear Failure: Common in timber beams along grain boundaries
  • Fastener Failure: In built-up sections where components are connected

Example Calculations

Example 1: Rectangular Section

A rectangular beam has width b = 100 mm and height h = 200 mm. Calculate the maximum shear stress for V = 50 kN.

Show Solution

For a rectangular section:

I = bh³/12 = 100 × 200³/12 = 66.67 × 10⁶ mm⁴

At the neutral axis: Q = b(h/2) × (h/4) = 100 × 100 × 50 = 500,000 mm³

τ_max = VQ/(Ib) = (50,000 × 500,000)/(66.67 × 10⁶ × 100) = 3.75 MPa

Note: For rectangular sections, τ_max = 1.5V/A = 1.5 × 50,000/(100 × 200) = 3.75 MPa ✓

Example 2: T-Section Analysis

A T-section has a flange width of 150 mm, flange thickness of 20 mm, web width of 15 mm, and total height of 180 mm. Find the shear stress at the flange-web junction for V = 75 kN.

Show Solution

First, locate the neutral axis:

Area_flange = 150 × 20 = 3000 mm²

Area_web = 15 × 160 = 2400 mm²

ȳ from bottom = (3000 × 170 + 2400 × 80)/5400 = 130 mm

Calculate I about neutral axis...

At junction: Q = 150 × 20 × (170 - 130) = 120,000 mm³

b at junction = 15 mm (web width)

τ = VQ/(Ib) = (75,000 × 120,000)/(I × 15)

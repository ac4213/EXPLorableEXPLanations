Beam Theory: Forces, Reactions and Internal Loads

Understanding the mechanical behaviour of loaded beams

Introduction to Beam Theory

Beams are fundamental structural elements that support loads primarily through bending. Understanding how beams respond to different loading scenarios is essential in mechanical and civil engineering design.

Beam Types and Support Conditions

Simply Supported Beam

A simply supported beam is supported at both ends. The supports allow:

  • Rotation at the supports
  • Vertical restraint at both ends
  • Horizontal movement at one end

These beams are statically determinate with two reaction forces.

Cantilever Beam

A cantilever beam is fixed at one end and free at the other. The fixed support:

  • Prevents rotation (moment restraint)
  • Prevents movement in all directions

These beams are also statically determinate with one reaction force and one reaction moment.

Equilibrium Conditions

For a beam to be in static equilibrium, the following conditions must be satisfied:

Sum of all forces in the vertical direction must equal zero:

\[\sum F_y = 0\]

Sum of all moments about any point must equal zero:

\[\sum M = 0\]

These principles allow us to determine the reaction forces at the supports when external loads are applied to the beam.

Simply Supported Beam - Reactions

Reaction Calculations for Simply Supported Beams

For a simply supported beam of length L with a concentrated force F at distance a from the left support:

Vertical reaction at right support (B):

\[B_y = \frac{F \cdot a + M}{L}\]

Vertical reaction at left support (A):

\[A_y = -F - B_y\]

Cantilever Beam - Reactions

Reaction Calculations for Cantilever Beams

For a cantilever beam of length L with a concentrated force F at distance a from the fixed end:

Moment reaction at the fixed end (A):

\[M_a = -(F \cdot a + M)\]

Vertical reaction at the fixed end (A):

\[A_y = -F\]

Section Method for Internal Loads

We can determine the internal loads in beams by considering the effect of all forces and moments on either side of a section.

Shear Force (V)

The shear force at any section is the sum of all transverse forces acting on either side of the section.

\[V(x) = \sum \text{forces to the left of section } x\]

The shear force diagram (SFD) shows how the shear force varies along the length of the beam.

Bending Moment (M)

The bending moment at any section is the sum of the moments of all forces acting on either side of the section.

\[M(x) = \sum \text{moments to the left of section } x\]

The bending moment diagram (BMD) shows how the bending moment varies along the length of the beam.

Important Beam Theorems

Derivative Relationships

The derivative of the bending moment is equal to the shear force

\[\frac{dM(x)}{dx} = V(x)\]

The derivative of the shear force is equal to the negative of the distributed load

\[\frac{dV(x)}{dx} = -q(x)\]

These equations show that:

  • The derivative of the bending moment is equal to the shear force
  • The derivative of the shear force is equal to the negative of the distributed load

Key Observations

  • The shear force is zero at points of maximum or minimum bending moment
  • The bending moment diagram is at an extreme value where the shear force changes sign
  • A concentrated force causes a step change in the shear force diagram
  • A concentrated moment causes a step change in the bending moment diagram
  • A distributed load causes a sloped line in the shear force diagram

Applications and Examples

Understanding internal loads and their analysis is fundamental across structural and mechanical engineering:

  • Building Design: Beams in floors, roofs, and frameworks must be sized to safely carry loads from occupants, equipment, and environmental forces like wind and snow.
  • Bridge Engineering: Analyzing shear forces and bending moments in bridge girders ensures safe passage of vehicles and trains. Critical sections are identified for detailed design.
  • Mechanical Components: Shafts, axles, and machine frames experience bending and shear during operation. Understanding internal loads prevents fatigue failures.
  • Aircraft Structures: Wings and fuselages are essentially beams subjected to complex loading. Shear and moment diagrams guide the placement of stringers and ribs.
  • Crane Design: Boom arms experience significant bending moments when lifting loads. Proper analysis ensures structural integrity and prevents catastrophic failure.
  • Automotive Engineering: Chassis frames act as beams supporting vehicle weight and road loads. Internal load analysis optimizes material usage while maintaining safety.

Design Considerations

Key factors in beam design based on internal load analysis:

  • Maximum Shear Force: Determines the required shear capacity and web design of the beam
  • Maximum Bending Moment: Determines the required flexural capacity and flange dimensions
  • Deflection Analysis: Based on the bending moment distribution to ensure serviceability
  • Material Selection: Chosen based on maximum stress values and fatigue considerations

Summary of Key Equations

  • Beam analysis starts with identifying support conditions and applied loads
  • Static equilibrium equations help determine the reaction forces
  • Shear force and bending moment diagrams reveal the internal loads throughout the beam
  • The relationship between load, shear, and moment helps in understanding beam behaviour

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Simply Supported Beam with Point Load

A simply supported beam of length L = 6 m has a point load P = 12 kN applied 2 m from the left support. Calculate:

  1. The reaction forces at both supports
  2. The shear force immediately to the left and right of the point load
  3. The maximum bending moment and its location

Show Solution

(a) Reaction forces:

Taking moments about the left support (A):

\[\sum M_A = 0: R_B \times 6 - 12 \times 2 = 0\]

\[R_B = \frac{24}{6} = 4 \text{ kN}\]

Vertical equilibrium:

\[\sum F_y = 0: R_A + R_B - 12 = 0\]

\[R_A = 12 - 4 = 8 \text{ kN}\]

(b) Shear force at point load:

Immediately to the left of P: \(V = R_A = 8 \text{ kN}\) (positive)

Immediately to the right of P: \(V = R_A - P = 8 - 12 = -4 \text{ kN}\) (negative)

The shear force has a step change of 12 kN at the load location.

(c) Maximum bending moment:

The maximum bending moment occurs where V = 0, which is at x = 2 m (at the load):

\[M_{max} = R_A \times 2 = 8 \times 2 = 16 \text{ kN·m}\]

Alternative calculation: \(M_{max} = R_B \times 4 = 4 \times 4 = 16 \text{ kN·m}\)

Problem 2: Cantilever Beam with Distributed Load

A cantilever beam of length L = 4 m is subjected to a uniformly distributed load w = 5 kN/m over its entire length. Determine:

  1. The reaction force and moment at the fixed support
  2. The shear force at x = 1 m from the fixed end
  3. The bending moment at the fixed end

Show Solution

(a) Reactions at fixed support:

Total load: \(W = w \times L = 5 \times 4 = 20 \text{ kN}\)

Vertical reaction: \(R_A = W = 20 \text{ kN}\) (upward)

Moment reaction: \(M_A = W \times \frac{L}{2} = 20 \times 2 = 40 \text{ kN·m}\) (counterclockwise)

(b) Shear force at x = 1 m:

For a cantilever with UDL, measuring x from the free end:

\[V(x) = -w \times x = -5 \times (4-1) = -15 \text{ kN}\]

Or measuring from fixed end: \(V = R_A - w \times 1 = 20 - 5 = 15 \text{ kN}\)

(c) Bending moment at fixed end:

\[M_{fixed} = -\frac{wL^2}{2} = -\frac{5 \times 4^2}{2} = -40 \text{ kN·m}\]

The negative sign indicates the moment creates tension on the top fibers (hogging moment).

Problem 3: Combined Loading - Point Load and UDL

A simply supported beam of length L = 5 m is subjected to:

  • A point load P = 10 kN at midspan (2.5 m from left support)
  • A uniformly distributed load w = 4 kN/m over the entire length

Calculate:

  1. The reaction forces at both supports
  2. The maximum shear force
  3. The maximum bending moment

Show Solution

(a) Reaction forces:

Total UDL: \(W = 4 \times 5 = 20 \text{ kN}\) acting at center (2.5 m)

By symmetry, both loads (P and W) act at midspan, so:

\[R_A = R_B = \frac{P + W}{2} = \frac{10 + 20}{2} = 15 \text{ kN}\]

(b) Maximum shear force:

Shear force diagram starts at \(V = R_A = 15 \text{ kN}\)

Just before midspan: \(V = 15 - 4 \times 2.5 = 15 - 10 = 5 \text{ kN}\)

Just after midspan: \(V = 5 - 10 = -5 \text{ kN}\)

Maximum shear force: \(|V_{max}| = 15 \text{ kN}\) (at supports)

(c) Maximum bending moment:

Maximum moment occurs at midspan where V = 0:

\[M_{max} = R_A \times 2.5 - \frac{w \times 2.5^2}{2}\]

\[M_{max} = 15 \times 2.5 - \frac{4 \times 6.25}{2} = 37.5 - 12.5 = 25 \text{ kN·m}\]

Alternative: \(M_{max} = \frac{PL}{4} + \frac{wL^2}{8} = \frac{10 \times 5}{4} + \frac{4 \times 25}{8} = 12.5 + 12.5 = 25 \text{ kN·m}\)

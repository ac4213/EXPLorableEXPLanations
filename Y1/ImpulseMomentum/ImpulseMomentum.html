Understanding Impulse and Momentum

The principle of impulse and momentum is a fundamental concept in dynamics that relates forces acting over time to changes in an object's motion. This principle provides powerful tools for analysing collisions, explosions, and other dynamic events.

Key Insight: Impulse is the effect of a force acting over time, whilst momentum is a measure of an object's motion. The impulse-momentum theorem states that the impulse applied to an object equals its change in momentum.

Linear Momentum

Linear momentum is defined as:

\(\vec{p} = m\vec{v}\)

Where:

  • \(\vec{p}\) = momentum vector (kg·m/s)
  • \(m\) = mass (kg)
  • \(\vec{v}\) = velocity vector (m/s)

Linear Impulse

Linear impulse is the integral of force over time:

\(\vec{J} = \displaystyle \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{F} \, dt\)

For constant force:

\(\vec{J} = \vec{F} \cdot \Delta t\)

The Impulse-Momentum Theorem

Starting from Newton's Second Law:

\(\vec{F} = m\vec{a} = m\displaystyle\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}\)

Integrating both sides with respect to time and recalling the definition of linear impulse above:

\(\displaystyle \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{F} \, dt = m \displaystyle\int_{v_1}^{v_2} d\vec{v}\)

\(\vec{J} = m\vec{v}_2 - m\vec{v}_1\)

\(\vec{J} = \Delta\vec{p}\)

Therefore: Impulse = Change in Momentum

Physical Interpretation: The impulse-momentum theorem tells us that to change an object's momentum, we must apply a force over time. A large force over a short time can produce the same momentum change as a small force over a long time.

NOTA BENE: both impulse and momentum are vector quantities, therefore they must be resolved into components when solving problems.

Conservation of Linear Momentum

For a system with no external forces:

\(\sum \vec{p}_{\text{initial}} = \sum \vec{p}_{\text{final}}\)

Or for a two-body collision:

\(m_1\vec{v}_{1i} + m_2\vec{v}_{2i} = m_1\vec{v}_{1f} + m_2\vec{v}_{2f}\)

Collisions and the Coefficient of Restitution

Types of Collisions

Collisions between objects can be classified based on how kinetic energy is conserved or lost during the impact:

1. Perfectly Elastic Collision (e = 1)

  • Both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved
  • No permanent deformation occurs
  • Objects separate after collision
  • Examples: Billiard balls (approximately), molecular collisions

2. Inelastic Collision (0 < e < 1)

  • Momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is NOT conserved
  • Some energy is converted to heat, sound, or deformation
  • Objects separate after collision but with reduced relative velocity
  • Examples: Most real-world collisions, tennis ball bouncing

3. Perfectly Inelastic Collision (e = 0)

  • Momentum is conserved but maximum kinetic energy is lost
  • Objects stick together after collision
  • Combined mass moves with common velocity
  • Examples: Clay balls colliding, car crashes where vehicles lock together

Coefficient of Restitution

The coefficient of restitution (e) is a measure of how "bouncy" a collision is. It quantifies the elasticity of the collision:

Definition:

\(e = \frac{\text{relative velocity of separation}}{\text{relative velocity of approach}}\)

Or mathematically:

\(e = -\frac{v_{2f} - v_{1f}}{v_{2i} - v_{1i}}\)

Where:

  • \(v_{1i}, v_{2i}\) = initial velocities of objects 1 and 2
  • \(v_{1f}, v_{2f}\) = final velocities of objects 1 and 2
  • The negative sign ensures e is positive

Physical Interpretation:

  • e = 1: Perfect bounce - objects separate with the same relative speed they approached
  • e = 0.5: Objects separate at half the relative speed they approached
  • e = 0: No bounce - objects stick together

Newton's Experimental Law

The coefficient of restitution was first studied by Isaac Newton, who found it depends on the materials of the colliding objects:

Typical Values:

  • Steel on steel: e ≈ 0.95
  • Glass on glass: e ≈ 0.94
  • Golf ball on club: e ≈ 0.83
  • Tennis ball on racquet: e ≈ 0.75
  • Basketball on floor: e ≈ 0.60
  • Putty or clay: e ≈ 0

Energy Loss in Collisions

The kinetic energy lost in a collision can be calculated using:

For a collision between two objects:

\(\Delta E_k = \frac{1}{2}\frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}(v_{1i} - v_{2i})^2(1 - e^2)\)

Note that:

  • When e = 1 (elastic): \(\Delta KE = 0\) (no energy lost)
  • When e = 0 (perfectly inelastic): Maximum energy loss

Oblique Collisions

For collisions that are not head-on, the coefficient of restitution applies only along the line of impact (normal direction):

The collision is analysed in two components:

  • Normal component: Along the line connecting centres at impact
  • Tangential component: Perpendicular to the line of impact

The coefficient of restitution equation applies only to the normal components:

\(e = -\frac{(v_{2n})_f - (v_{1n})_f}{(v_{2n})_i - (v_{1n})_i}\)

Tangential components are unchanged (assuming no friction)

Important Note: In all types of collisions, momentum is ALWAYS conserved (in isolated systems). The coefficient of restitution tells us how kinetic energy behaves, not momentum!

Engineering Applications of Restitution

Understanding the coefficient of restitution is crucial for engineering design:

Impact Force and Time:

For a given momentum change, the average impact force depends on collision time:

\(F_{avg} = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}\)

  • High e (elastic): Shorter contact time → Higher peak forces
  • Low e (inelastic): Longer contact time → Lower peak forces

Design Considerations:

  • Safety Equipment: Helmets, airbags use low e materials to extend impact time and reduce forces
  • Sports Equipment: Golf balls, tennis balls designed with specific e values for optimal performance
  • Industrial Dampers: Shock absorbers use very low e to dissipate energy
  • Precision Machinery: High e materials minimise energy loss in mechanisms

Angular Momentum and Angular Impulse

Angular Momentum

For a particle:

\(\vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p} = \vec{r} \times m\vec{v}\)

(where \(\times\) denotes the vector product between two vectors)

For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis it can be proven that:

\(L = I\omega\)

Where:

  • \(I\quad\) moment of inertia (kg·m²)
  • \(\omega\quad\) angular velocity (rad/s)

Angular Impulse

Angular impulse is the integral of torque/moment over time:

\(\vec{J}_\theta = \displaystyle\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{M} \, dt\)

For constant torque/moment:

\(\vec{J}_\theta = \vec{M} \cdot \Delta t\)

Derivation from Rotational Dynamics

Starting from the rotational form of Newton's Second Law:

\(\vec{M} = I\vec{\alpha} = I\displaystyle\frac{d\vec{\omega}}{dt}\)

Integrating both sides:

\(\displaystyle\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{M} \, dt = I\int_{\omega_1}^{\omega_2} d\vec{\omega}\)

\(\vec{J}_\theta = I\vec{\omega}_2 - I\vec{\omega}_1 = \Delta\vec{L}\)

Therefore: Angular Impulse = Change in Angular Momentum

Conservation of Angular Momentum

When no external torques act on a system:

\(\vec{L}_{\text{initial}} = \vec{L}_{\text{final}}\)

or:

\(I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2\)

Classic Example: A figure skater spins faster when pulling their arms inward. As the moment of inertia \(I\) decreases, the angular velocity \(\omega\) must increase to conserve angular momentum.

Real-World Applications

Linear Impulse and Momentum

  • Automotive Safety: Crumple zones and airbags increase collision time, reducing the force on passengers
  • Sports: Follow-through in golf, tennis, and cricket increases contact time and impulse
  • Aerospace: Rocket propulsion uses momentum conservation (thrust = mass flow rate × exhaust velocity)
  • Ballistics: Recoil of firearms demonstrates momentum conservation

Angular Impulse and Momentum

  • Satellite Stabilisation: Reaction wheels and gyroscopes maintain orientation
  • Sports: Divers and gymnasts change their rotation rate by altering body position
  • Engineering: Flywheels store rotational energy and smooth out torque variations
  • Helicopters: Tail rotors counteract the torque from the main rotor

Advanced Concepts

Oblique Collisions

For 2D collisions at an angle, momentum is conserved in both x and y directions independently:

X-direction: \(m_1v_{1xi} + m_2v_{2xi} = m_1v_{1xf} + m_2v_{2xf}\)

Y-direction: \(m_1v_{1yi} + m_2v_{2yi} = m_1v_{1yf} + m_2v_{2yf}\)

Variable Mass Systems

For systems with changing mass (like rockets):

Rocket equation (Tsiolkovsky):

\(\Delta v = v_e \ln\left(\frac{m_0}{m_f}\right)\)

Where:

  • \(v_e\) = exhaust velocity
  • \(m_0\) = initial mass
  • \(m_f\) = final mass

Gyroscopic Effects

When a spinning object experiences a torque perpendicular to its angular momentum, it precesses:

Precession rate: \(\Omega = \frac{\tau}{L} = \frac{\tau}{I\omega}\)

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Head-on Collision

Two cars collide head-on. Car A (1500 kg) travels at 20 m/s east, whilst Car B (1200 kg) travels at 15 m/s west. If they stick together after collision, find their final velocity.

Show Solution

Define east as positive direction.

Initial momentum: \(p_i = m_Av_A + m_Bv_B = 1500(20) + 1200(-15) = 30000 - 18000 = 12000 \text{ kg·m/s}\)

After collision (perfectly inelastic): \(p_f = (m_A + m_B)v_f\)

By conservation: \(12000 = (1500 + 1200)v_f = 2700v_f\)

\(v_f = \frac{12000}{2700} = 4.44 \text{ m/s east}\)

Problem 2: Figure Skater

A figure skater with arms extended has a moment of inertia of 2.5 kg·m² and rotates at 3 rad/s. When pulling arms in, the moment of inertia reduces to 1.0 kg·m². Find the new angular velocity.

Show Solution

By conservation of angular momentum:

\(L_i = L_f\)

\(I_i\omega_i = I_f\omega_f\)

\(2.5 \times 3 = 1.0 \times \omega_f\)

\(\omega_f = 7.5 \text{ rad/s}\)

The skater spins 2.5 times faster!

Problem 3: Impulse and Force

A 0.15 kg cricket ball travelling at 40 m/s is struck by a bat and leaves at 50 m/s in the opposite direction. If contact time is 0.003 s, find the average force on the ball.

Show Solution

Define initial direction as positive:

Initial momentum: \(p_i = 0.15 \times 40 = 6 \text{ kg·m/s}\)

Final momentum: \(p_f = 0.15 \times (-50) = -7.5 \text{ kg·m/s}\)

Change in momentum: \(\Delta p = -7.5 - 6 = -13.5 \text{ kg·m/s}\)

Impulse: \(J = F_{avg} \times \Delta t = \Delta p\)

\(F_{avg} = \frac{-13.5}{0.003} = -4500 \text{ N}\)

The force is 4500 N in the opposite direction to initial motion.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  1. Impulse-Momentum Theorem: \(\vec{J} = \Delta\vec{p}\quad\) Impulse equals change in momentum
  2. Conservation of Momentum: In isolated systems, total momentum is conserved
  3. Angular Analogue: \(\vec{J}_\theta = \Delta\vec{L}\quad\) Angular impulse equals change in angular momentum
  4. Conservation of Angular Momentum: Without external torques, angular momentum is conserved
  5. Practical Applications: These principles are crucial for collision analysis, propulsion systems, and rotational dynamics

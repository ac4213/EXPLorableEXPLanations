Introduction to Fatigue Analysis

Fatigue is a failure mechanism that occurs in materials subjected to cyclic loading, even when the stresses are well below the material's ultimate tensile strength. This phenomenon accounts for a significant percentage of mechanical failures in engineering components.

Key aspects that differentiate fatigue from static failure:

  • Failure occurs after many loading cycles, not immediately
  • Failure can occur at stress levels well below the yield strength
  • Failure typically begins at a localized defect or stress concentration
  • The process involves three stages: crack initiation, crack propagation, and final fracture

This interactive module explores the fundamental principles of stress-based fatigue analysis, providing visualizations and simulations to develop your intuition about this complex failure mode.

Fundamentals of Fatigue Loading

Stress Cycle Parameters

In fatigue analysis, we characterize cyclic loading using several key parameters:

\[\sigma_{max} = \text{Maximum stress in cycle}\] \[\sigma_{min} = \text{Minimum stress in cycle}\] \[\sigma_a = \frac{\sigma_{max} - \sigma_{min}}{2} = \text{Stress amplitude}\] \[\sigma_m = \frac{\sigma_{max} + \sigma_{min}}{2} = \text{Mean stress}\] \[R = \frac{\sigma_{min}}{\sigma_{max}} = \text{Stress ratio}\]

Common Types of Fatigue Loading

  • Fully Reversed Loading (R = -1): Equal tension and compression, with mean stress = 0
  • Zero-to-Max Loading (R = 0): Cycles from zero to a maximum value, common in pressurized systems
  • Fluctuating Tension (0 < R < 1): Cycling occurs entirely in tension
  • Fluctuating Compression (R > 1 or R < -1): Cycling occurs entirely in compression

S-N Curves and Fatigue Life Prediction

The Wöhler Experiment

August Wöhler conducted the first systematic investigation of fatigue failure in the 1850s while studying railway axle failures. His experiments involved applying cyclic loads to test specimens until failure and plotting the results.

The resulting S-N curve (Stress vs Number of cycles) represents the relationship between stress amplitude and the number of cycles to failure for a specific mean stress (often zero).

Basquin's Equation

The relationship between stress amplitude and fatigue life can be described by Basquin's equation:

\[\sigma_a = \sigma'_f (2N_f)^b\]

Where:

  • \(\sigma_a\) = Stress amplitude
  • \(\sigma'_f\) = Fatigue strength coefficient
  • \(N_f\) = Number of cycles to failure
  • \(b\) = Fatigue strength exponent (typically between -0.05 and -0.12)

Characteristics of S-N Curves

  • Ferrous Metals: Typically exhibit a distinct endurance limit (horizontal asymptote)
  • Non-ferrous Metals: Generally do not show a distinct endurance limit and continue to decline
  • High Cycle Fatigue: Typically above 10³ cycles, elastic deformation dominates
  • Low Cycle Fatigue: Below 10³ cycles, plastic deformation is significant

Effect of Mean Stress on Fatigue Life

In practical applications, many components experience cyclic loading with a non-zero mean stress. Tensile mean stress generally reduces fatigue life, while compressive mean stress tends to improve it.

Mean Stress Correction Models

Various models exist to account for mean stress effects:

\[\text{Goodman: } \frac{\sigma_a}{S_e} + \frac{\sigma_m}{S_{ut}} = 1\] \[\text{Gerber: } \frac{\sigma_a}{S_e} + \left(\frac{\sigma_m}{S_{ut}}\right)^2 = 1\] \[\text{Soderberg: } \frac{\sigma_a}{S_e} + \frac{\sigma_m}{S_y} = 1\] \[\text{Modified Goodman: } \frac{\sigma_a}{S_e} + \frac{\sigma_m}{S_{ut}} \leq 1 \text{ and } \sigma_{max} \leq S_y\]

Where:

  • \(S_e\) = Endurance limit for fully reversed loading
  • \(S_{ut}\) = Ultimate tensile strength
  • \(S_y\) = Yield strength

Interpreting the Haigh Diagram

  • Points inside the boundary represent safe operation
  • Points on the boundary represent failure at the specified number of cycles
  • Points outside the boundary exceed the fatigue failure criteria
  • The diagonal line represents the yield criterion where σmax = Sy

Selecting a Mean Stress Correction Model

  • Goodman: Conservative for most metals, widely used in industry
  • Gerber: Better fit for ductile materials, less conservative
  • Soderberg: Most conservative, considers yielding
  • Modified Goodman: Accounts for both fatigue and static yielding

Cumulative Damage Theory

In real applications, components often experience variable amplitude loading rather than constant amplitude cycles. The Palmgren-Miner rule provides a way to assess cumulative damage.

Palmgren-Miner Linear Damage Rule

This rule states that the damage fraction at any stress level is linearly proportional to the number of cycles applied divided by the number of cycles to failure at that stress level.

\[D = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{n_i}{N_i}\]

Where:

  • \(D\) = Cumulative damage (failure occurs when D ≥ 1)
  • \(n_i\) = Number of cycles applied at stress level i
  • \(N_i\) = Number of cycles to failure at stress level i
  • \(k\) = Number of different stress levels

Limitations of the Palmgren-Miner Rule

  • Does not account for sequence effects (high-low vs low-high stress sequences)
  • Does not consider stress interactions
  • Assumes linear damage accumulation
  • Observed failure often occurs at D values between 0.7 and 1.5
  • Conservative design often uses a target D value of 0.7 or lower

Solved Problems

Problem 1: Fatigue Life Prediction

Problem: Three mechanical components made of the same steel alloy (fatigue strength coefficient σ'f = 1000 MPa, fatigue strength exponent b = -0.09) are subjected to different cyclic loading conditions:

  • Component A: Stress varies between +300 MPa and -300 MPa
  • Component B: Stress varies between +400 MPa and 0 MPa
  • Component C: Stress varies between +250 MPa and +50 MPa

Classify each loading type, calculate the stress parameters, and determine which component will fail first using Basquin's equation.

Solution:

Component A:

  • Maximum stress: σmax = +300 MPa
  • Minimum stress: σmin = -300 MPa
  • Stress amplitude: σa = (σmax - σmin)/2 = (300 - (-300))/2 = 300 MPa
  • Mean stress: σm = (σmax + σmin)/2 = (300 + (-300))/2 = 0 MPa
  • Stress ratio: R = σmin/σmax = -300/300 = -1
  • Loading type: Fully reversed loading (R = -1, σm = 0)

Component B:

  • Maximum stress: σmax = +400 MPa
  • Minimum stress: σmin = 0 MPa
  • Stress amplitude: σa = (σmax - σmin)/2 = (400 - 0)/2 = 200 MPa
  • Mean stress: σm = (σmax + σmin)/2 = (400 + 0)/2 = 200 MPa
  • Stress ratio: R = σmin/σmax = 0/400 = 0
  • Loading type: Zero-to-max loading (R = 0)

Component C:

  • Maximum stress: σmax = +250 MPa
  • Minimum stress: σmin = +50 MPa
  • Stress amplitude: σa = (σmax - σmin)/2 = (250 - 50)/2 = 100 MPa
  • Mean stress: σm = (σmax + σmin)/2 = (250 + 50)/2 = 150 MPa
  • Stress ratio: R = σmin/σmax = 50/250 = 0.2
  • Loading type: Fluctuating tension (0 < R < 1)

Fatigue Life Calculation:

Using Basquin's equation: σa = σ'f(2Nf)^b

For Component A (fully reversed, mean stress correction not needed):

\[300 = 1000 \times (2N_f)^{-0.09}\]

\[\frac{300}{1000} = (2N_f)^{-0.09}\]

\[0.3 = (2N_f)^{-0.09}\]

\[(2N_f) = 0.3^{-1/0.09} = 0.3^{-11.11} = 1.38 \times 10^5\]

\[N_{f,A} = 6.90 \times 10^4 \text{ cycles}\]

For Component B (zero-to-max, requires mean stress correction):

Using Goodman relation: \[\sigma_{a,eq} = \frac{\sigma_a}{1 - \sigma_m/S_u}\]

Assuming Su = 800 MPa for this steel:

\[\sigma_{a,eq} = \frac{200}{1 - 200/800} = \frac{200}{0.75} = 267 \text{ MPa}\]

\[267 = 1000 \times (2N_f)^{-0.09}\]

\[0.267 = (2N_f)^{-0.09}\]

\[(2N_f) = 0.267^{-1/0.09} = 0.267^{-11.11} = 2.47 \times 10^5\]

\[N_{f,B} = 1.24 \times 10^5 \text{ cycles}\]

For Component C (fluctuating tension, requires mean stress correction):

\[\sigma_{a,eq} = \frac{100}{1 - 150/800} = \frac{100}{0.8125} = 123.1 \text{ MPa}\]

\[123.1 = 1000 \times (2N_f)^{-0.09}\]

\[0.1231 = (2N_f)^{-0.09}\]

\[(2N_f) = 0.1231^{-1/0.09} = 0.1231^{-11.11} = 4.02 \times 10^6\]

\[N_{f,C} = 2.01 \times 10^6 \text{ cycles}\]

Conclusion:

The fatigue lives of the three components are:

  • Component A: 6.90 × 10⁴ cycles
  • Component B: 1.24 × 10⁵ cycles
  • Component C: 2.01 × 10⁶ cycles

Component A will fail first, despite having the same stress amplitude as Component B, because it experiences a fully reversed load cycle with double the stress range. Component C will last the longest due to its lower stress amplitude, despite having a positive mean stress.

Problem 2: Safety Factor for Variable Amplitude Loading

Problem: A mechanical component made of AISI 4340 steel (Su = 1200 MPa, Sy = 1000 MPa, Se = 500 MPa) experiences the following stress history:

  • Stress Block 1: σa = 300 MPa, σm = 200 MPa, 10,000 cycles
  • Stress Block 2: σa = 250 MPa, σm = 150 MPa, 30,000 cycles
  • Stress Block 3: σa = 400 MPa, σm = 100 MPa, 5,000 cycles

Calculate the cumulative damage using Palmgren-Miner's rule with the Goodman mean stress correction. What factor of safety exists against fatigue failure?

Solution:

  1. Convert each stress block to equivalent fully-reversed stress amplitude using Goodman relation: \[\sigma_{a,eq} = \frac{\sigma_a}{1 - \sigma_m/S_u}\] For Block 1: \[\sigma_{a,eq,1} = \frac{300}{1 - 200/1200} = \frac{300}{0.833} = 360 \text{ MPa}\] For Block 2: \[\sigma_{a,eq,2} = \frac{250}{1 - 150/1200} = \frac{250}{0.875} = 286 \text{ MPa}\] For Block 3: \[\sigma_{a,eq,3} = \frac{400}{1 - 100/1200} = \frac{400}{0.917} = 436 \text{ MPa}\]
  2. Calculate the number of cycles to failure for each equivalent stress amplitude using Basquin's equation: \[\sigma_{a,eq} = \sigma'_f (2N_f)^b\] Assuming fatigue strength coefficient \(\sigma'_f = 1500\) MPa and \(b = -0.09\): For Block 1: \[360 = 1500 \times (2N_{f,1})^{-0.09}\] \[N_{f,1} = 67,920 \text{ cycles}\] For Block 2: \[286 = 1500 \times (2N_{f,2})^{-0.09}\] \[N_{f,2} = 223,810 \text{ cycles}\] For Block 3: \[436 = 1500 \times (2N_{f,3})^{-0.09}\] \[N_{f,3} = 25,180 \text{ cycles}\]
  3. Apply Palmgren-Miner's rule to calculate cumulative damage: \[D = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{n_i}{N_i} = \frac{10,000}{67,920} + \frac{30,000}{223,810} + \frac{5,000}{25,180} = 0.147 + 0.134 + 0.199 = 0.480\]
  4. Calculate the factor of safety against fatigue failure: \[FS = \frac{1}{D} = \frac{1}{0.480} = 2.08\]

Conclusion: The cumulative damage is 0.480, which is less than 1, indicating that the component should not fail under the given loading conditions. The factor of safety against fatigue failure is 2.08, meaning the component could theoretically withstand approximately twice the applied loading cycles before failure would be expected.

Problem 3: Design for Infinite Life

Problem: A machine component made of SAE 1045 steel (Su = 650 MPa, Sy = 530 MPa) will be subjected to fluctuating stresses with a mean stress of 100 MPa. The endurance limit for fully reversed loading (σm = 0) is 300 MPa. If the component is to be designed for infinite life with a factor of safety of 1.5, determine the maximum allowable stress amplitude using:

  1. Goodman criterion
  2. Gerber criterion
  3. Soderberg criterion

Solution:

First, let's adjust the endurance limit by the factor of safety:

\[S_e = \frac{300}{1.5} = 200 \text{ MPa}\]

a) Using the Goodman criterion:

\[\frac{\sigma_a}{S_e} + \frac{\sigma_m}{S_u} = 1\] \[\frac{\sigma_a}{200} + \frac{100}{650} = 1\] \[\frac{\sigma_a}{200} = 1 - \frac{100}{650} = 1 - 0.154 = 0.846\] \[\sigma_a = 200 \times 0.846 = 169.2 \text{ MPa}\]

b) Using the Gerber criterion:

\[\frac{\sigma_a}{S_e} + \left(\frac{\sigma_m}{S_u}\right)^2 = 1\] \[\frac{\sigma_a}{200} + \left(\frac{100}{650}\right)^2 = 1\] \[\frac{\sigma_a}{200} = 1 - \left(\frac{100}{650}\right)^2 = 1 - 0.024 = 0.976\] \[\sigma_a = 200 \times 0.976 = 195.2 \text{ MPa}\]

c) Using the Soderberg criterion:

\[\frac{\sigma_a}{S_e} + \frac{\sigma_m}{S_y} = 1\] \[\frac{\sigma_a}{200} + \frac{100}{530} = 1\] \[\frac{\sigma_a}{200} = 1 - \frac{100}{530} = 1 - 0.189 = 0.811\] \[\sigma_a = 200 \times 0.811 = 162.2 \text{ MPa}\]

Conclusion: For a design with a factor of safety of 1.5 and a mean stress of 100 MPa, the maximum allowable stress amplitudes are:

  • Goodman criterion: 169.2 MPa
  • Gerber criterion: 195.2 MPa
  • Soderberg criterion: 162.2 MPa

The Gerber criterion gives the highest allowable stress amplitude because it is the least conservative of the three criteria. The Soderberg criterion results in the lowest allowable stress amplitude because it is the most conservative, taking into account the possibility of yielding.

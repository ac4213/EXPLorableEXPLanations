3D Stress and Strain Theory

Cauchy Stress Tensor

The Cauchy stress tensor is a second-order tensor used to represent the state of stress at a point within a material. In three dimensions, it is represented as a 3×3 matrix:

$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} & \tau_{xy} & \tau_{xz} \\ \tau_{yx} & \sigma_{yy} & \tau_{yz} \\ \tau_{zx} & \tau_{zy} & \sigma_{zz} \end{bmatrix}$

Where:

  • \(\sigma_{xx}\), \(\sigma_{yy}\), \(\sigma_{zz}\) are normal stresses
  • \(\tau_{xy}\), \(\tau_{yz}\), \(\tau_{xz}\), \(\tau_{yx}\), \(\tau_{zy}\), \(\tau_{zx}\) are shear stresses

Due to moment equilibrium, the stress tensor is symmetric, meaning \(\tau_{ij} = \tau_{ji}\).

The stress tensor completely defines the state of stress at a point. Each component represents the stress (force per unit area) acting on a plane perpendicular to one axis in the direction of another axis.

Green-Lagrange Strain Tensor

The Green-Lagrange strain tensor describes the deformation of a material from an initial to a deformed state. It is defined as:

$\mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{F}^T\mathbf{F} - \mathbf{I})$

Where:

  • \(\mathbf{E}\) is the Green-Lagrange strain tensor
  • \(\mathbf{F}\) is the deformation gradient tensor
  • \(\mathbf{I}\) is the identity tensor

For small deformations, the strain tensor can be approximated as:

$$\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} = \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_{xx} & \frac{1}{2}\gamma_{xy} & \frac{1}{2}\gamma_{xz} \\ \frac{1}{2}\gamma_{yx} & \varepsilon_{yy} & \frac{1}{2}\gamma_{yz} \\ \frac{1}{2}\gamma_{zx} & \frac{1}{2}\gamma_{zy} & \varepsilon_{zz} \end{bmatrix}$$

Where:

  • \(\varepsilon_{xx}\), \(\varepsilon_{yy}\), \(\varepsilon_{zz}\) are normal strains
  • \(\gamma_{xy}\), \(\gamma_{yz}\), \(\gamma_{xz}\) are engineering shear strains

Constitutive Models

Constitutive models describe the relationship between stress and strain in a material. The general form follows Hooke's law:

$$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \mathbf{C} : \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$$

Where \(\mathbf{C}\) is the fourth-order stiffness tensor.

Isotropic Materials

For isotropic materials (properties are the same in all directions), the constitutive equation can be written as:

$$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \lambda \text{tr}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}) \mathbf{I} + 2\mu \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$$

Where:

  • \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\) are Lamé parameters
  • \(\text{tr}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})\) is the trace of the strain tensor

Alternatively, using engineering constants:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_{xx} \\ \varepsilon_{yy} \\ \varepsilon_{zz} \\ \gamma_{xy} \\ \gamma_{yz} \\ \gamma_{xz} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -\frac{\nu}{E} & \frac{1}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -\frac{\nu}{E} & -\frac{\nu}{E} & \frac{1}{E} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} \\ \sigma_{yy} \\ \sigma_{zz} \\ \tau_{xy} \\ \tau_{yz} \\ \tau_{xz} \end{bmatrix}$$

Orthotropic Materials

For orthotropic materials (different properties in three perpendicular directions), the compliance matrix becomes:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_{xx} \\ \varepsilon_{yy} \\ \varepsilon_{zz} \\ \gamma_{xy} \\ \gamma_{yz} \\ \gamma_{xz} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{E_x} & -\frac{\nu_{xy}}{E_x} & -\frac{\nu_{xz}}{E_x} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -\frac{\nu_{yx}}{E_y} & \frac{1}{E_y} & -\frac{\nu_{yz}}{E_y} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -\frac{\nu_{zx}}{E_z} & -\frac{\nu_{zy}}{E_z} & \frac{1}{E_z} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G_{xy}} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G_{yz}} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G_{xz}} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} \\ \sigma_{yy} \\ \sigma_{zz} \\ \tau_{xy} \\ \tau_{yz} \\ \tau_{xz} \end{bmatrix}$$

Transversely Isotropic Materials

For transversely isotropic materials (isotropic in one plane), the compliance matrix simplifies. Assuming the plane of isotropy is the x-y plane:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_{xx} \\ \varepsilon_{yy} \\ \varepsilon_{zz} \\ \gamma_{xy} \\ \gamma_{yz} \\ \gamma_{xz} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{E_T} & -\frac{\nu_T}{E_T} & -\frac{\nu_{TL}}{E_T} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -\frac{\nu_T}{E_T} & \frac{1}{E_T} & -\frac{\nu_{TL}}{E_T} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -\frac{\nu_{LT}}{E_L} & -\frac{\nu_{LT}}{E_L} & \frac{1}{E_L} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{2(1+\nu_T)}{E_T} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G_{LT}} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{1}{G_{LT}} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} \\ \sigma_{yy} \\ \sigma_{zz} \\ \tau_{xy} \\ \tau_{yz} \\ \tau_{xz} \end{bmatrix}$$

Where:

  • \(E_T\) is the Young's modulus in the transverse direction (x-y plane)
  • \(E_L\) is the Young's modulus in the longitudinal direction (z-axis)
  • \(\nu_T\) is Poisson's ratio in the transverse plane
  • \(\nu_{LT}\) and \(\nu_{TL}\) are the Poisson's ratios for the longitudinal-transverse interactions
  • \(G_{LT}\) is the longitudinal-transverse shear modulus

3D Stress Transformation

Stress transformation allows us to determine the stress components on a plane with any orientation. For 3D transformation, we use direction cosines to define the orientation of the new coordinate system.

$$\boldsymbol{\sigma}' = \mathbf{Q}^T \boldsymbol{\sigma} \mathbf{Q}$$

Where:

  • \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}'\) is the transformed stress tensor
  • \(\mathbf{Q}\) is the transformation matrix (composed of direction cosines)

Principal stresses are the eigenvalues of the stress tensor, representing the normal stresses on planes where shear stresses vanish. The principal directions are the eigenvectors of the stress tensor.

The characteristic equation for finding principal stresses is:

$$\det(\boldsymbol{\sigma} - \sigma_I \mathbf{I}) = 0$$

Which expands to:

$$\sigma^3 - I_1\sigma^2 + I_2\sigma - I_3 = 0$$

Where \(I_1\), \(I_2\), and \(I_3\) are the first, second, and third invariants of the stress tensor.

Failure Criteria

Maximum Principal Stress Theory (Rankine)

Failure occurs when the maximum principal stress reaches the yield strength of the material:

$$\max(|\sigma_1|, |\sigma_2|, |\sigma_3|) \geq \sigma_y$$

Maximum Shear Stress Theory (Tresca)

Failure occurs when the maximum shear stress reaches a critical value:

$$\max(|\sigma_1 - \sigma_2|, |\sigma_2 - \sigma_3|, |\sigma_3 - \sigma_1|) \geq 2\tau_y = \sigma_y$$

Von Mises Criterion

Failure occurs when the distortion energy reaches a critical value:

$$\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}[(\sigma_1 - \sigma_2)^2 + (\sigma_2 - \sigma_3)^2 + (\sigma_3 - \sigma_1)^2]} \geq \sigma_y$$

Or, equivalently:

$$\sqrt{(\sigma_x - \sigma_y)^2 + (\sigma_y - \sigma_z)^2 + (\sigma_z - \sigma_x)^2 + 6(\tau_{xy}^2 + \tau_{yz}^2 + \tau_{xz}^2)} \geq \sqrt{2}\sigma_y$$

The von Mises criterion is commonly used for ductile materials, while the Rankine criterion is often applied to brittle materials. The Tresca criterion is more conservative than von Mises but simpler to calculate.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Principal Stresses

For the stress tensor below, calculate the principal stresses and their directions:

$$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} 80 & 25 & 0 \\ 25 & 40 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 20 \end{bmatrix} \text{ MPa}$$
Show Solution

To find the principal stresses, we need to solve the characteristic equation:

\(\det(\boldsymbol{\sigma} - \sigma_I \mathbf{I}) = 0\)

This gives us:

\((\sigma - 20)[(\sigma - 80)(\sigma - 40) - 25^2] = 0\)

\((\sigma - 20)[\sigma^2 - 120\sigma + 3200 - 625] = 0\)

\((\sigma - 20)(\sigma^2 - 120\sigma + 2575) = 0\)

Solving this equation:

\(\sigma_1 = 20 \text{ MPa}\)

\(\sigma_{2,3} = \frac{120 \pm \sqrt{120^2 - 4 \times 2575}}{2} = \frac{120 \pm \sqrt{14400 - 10300}}{2} = \frac{120 \pm \sqrt{4100}}{2} = \frac{120 \pm 64}{2}\)

\(\sigma_2 = 92 \text{ MPa}\)

\(\sigma_3 = 28 \text{ MPa}\)

The principal directions can be found by substituting each principal stress back into \((\boldsymbol{\sigma} - \sigma_I \mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) and solving for the eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\).

Problem 2: Failure Analysis

A component made of steel (yield strength \(\sigma_y = 250 \text{ MPa}\)) is subjected to the following stress state:

$$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} 150 & 50 & 30 \\ 50 & 100 & 20 \\ 30 & 20 & 50 \end{bmatrix} \text{ MPa}$$

Determine whether the component will fail according to:

  1. Maximum Principal Stress Theory (Rankine)
  2. Maximum Shear Stress Theory (Tresca)
  3. Von Mises Criterion
Show Solution

First, let's calculate the principal stresses by finding the eigenvalues of the stress tensor. Using numerical methods:

\(\sigma_1 = 175.7 \text{ MPa}\)

\(\sigma_2 = 89.5 \text{ MPa}\)

\(\sigma_3 = 34.8 \text{ MPa}\)

a) Maximum Principal Stress Theory (Rankine):

\(\max(|\sigma_1|, |\sigma_2|, |\sigma_3|) = 175.7 \text{ MPa} < \sigma_y = 250 \text{ MPa}\)

The component will not fail according to this criterion.

b) Maximum Shear Stress Theory (Tresca):

\(\max(|\sigma_1 - \sigma_2|, |\sigma_2 - \sigma_3|, |\sigma_3 - \sigma_1|) = |\sigma_1 - \sigma_3| = |175.7 - 34.8| = 140.9 \text{ MPa}\)

\(\tau_{max} = \frac{140.9}{2} = 70.45 \text{ MPa} < \tau_y = \frac{\sigma_y}{2} = 125 \text{ MPa}\)

The component will not fail according to this criterion.

c) Von Mises Criterion:

\(\sigma_{vm} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}[(\sigma_1 - \sigma_2)^2 + (\sigma_2 - \sigma_3)^2 + (\sigma_3 - \sigma_1)^2]}\)

\(\sigma_{vm} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}[(175.7 - 89.5)^2 + (89.5 - 34.8)^2 + (34.8 - 175.7)^2]}\)

\(\sigma_{vm} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}[7442.4 + 2990.2 + 19846.8]} = \sqrt{\frac{30279.4}{2}} = 123.0 \text{ MPa} < \sigma_y = 250 \text{ MPa}\)

The component will not fail according to this criterion either.

Problem 3: Stress Transformation

Given the stress tensor:

$$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} 60 & 15 & 25 \\ 15 & 40 & 10 \\ 25 & 10 & 20 \end{bmatrix} \text{ MPa}$$

Calculate the stress components in a coordinate system rotated 30° about the z-axis.

Show Solution

For rotation about the z-axis by 30°, the transformation matrix is:

\(\mathbf{Q} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(30°) & \sin(30°) & 0 \\ -\sin(30°) & \cos(30°) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.866 & 0.5 & 0 \\ -0.5 & 0.866 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

The transformed stress tensor is:

\(\boldsymbol{\sigma}' = \mathbf{Q}^T \boldsymbol{\sigma} \mathbf{Q}\)

Computing this matrix multiplication:

\(\boldsymbol{\sigma}' = \begin{bmatrix} 0.866 & -0.5 & 0 \\ 0.5 & 0.866 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 60 & 15 & 25 \\ 15 & 40 & 10 \\ 25 & 10 & 20 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.866 & 0.5 & 0 \\ -0.5 & 0.866 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

After performing the matrix multiplication:

\(\boldsymbol{\sigma}' = \begin{bmatrix} 47.7 & 32.5 & 25.8 \\ 32.5 & 52.3 & 5.7 \\ 25.8 & 5.7 & 20 \end{bmatrix} \text{ MPa}\)

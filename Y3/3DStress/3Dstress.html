3D Stress and Strain Theory

Cauchy Stress Tensor

The Cauchy stress tensor is a second-order tensor used to represent the state of stress at a point within a material. In three dimensions, it is represented as a 3×3 matrix:

\[\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{xx} & \tau_{xy} & \tau_{xz} \\ \tau_{yx} & \sigma_{yy} & \tau_{yz} \\ \tau_{zx} & \tau_{zy} & \sigma_{zz} \end{bmatrix}\]

Where:

  • σxx, σyy, σzz are normal stresses
  • τxy, τyz, τxz, τyx, τzy, τzx are shear stresses

Due to moment equilibrium, the stress tensor is symmetric, meaning τij = τji.

The stress tensor completely defines the state of stress at a point. Each component represents the stress (force per unit area) acting on a plane perpendicular to one axis in the direction of another axis.

Green-Lagrange Strain Tensor

The Green-Lagrange strain tensor describes the deformation of a material from an initial to a deformed state. For small strains, it simplifies to the infinitesimal strain tensor:

\[\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} = \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_{xx} & \gamma_{xy}/2 & \gamma_{xz}/2 \\ \gamma_{xy}/2 & \varepsilon_{yy} & \gamma_{yz}/2 \\ \gamma_{xz}/2 & \gamma_{yz}/2 & \varepsilon_{zz} \end{bmatrix}\]

Where ε represents normal strains and γ represents engineering shear strains.

Failure Criteria

Several criteria predict material failure under complex stress states:

Von Mises Criterion

\[\sigma_{VM} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}[(σ_1-σ_2)^2 + (σ_2-σ_3)^2 + (σ_3-σ_1)^2]}\]

Failure occurs when σVM ≥ σy (yield strength)

Tresca Criterion

\[\tau_{max} = \frac{σ_{max} - σ_{min}}{2}\]

Failure occurs when τmax ≥ σy/2

The von Mises criterion is based on distortion energy theory and is widely used for ductile materials. The Tresca criterion is more conservative than von Mises but simpler to calculate.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Principal Stresses

For the stress tensor below, calculate the principal stresses and their directions:

\[\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} 80 & 25 & 0 \\ 25 & 40 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 20 \end{bmatrix} \text{ MPa}\]
Show Solution

To find the principal stresses, we need to solve the characteristic equation:

\(\det(\boldsymbol{\lambda} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = 0\)

This gives us:

\((\lambda - 20)[(\lambda - 80)(\lambda - 40) - 25^2] = 0\)

\((\lambda - 20)[\lambda^2 - 120\lambda + 3200 - 625] = 0\)

\((\lambda - 20)(\lambda^2 - 120\lambda + 2575) = 0\)

Solving this equation:

\(\lambda_1 = 20 \text{ MPa}\)

\(\lambda_{2,3} = \frac{120 \pm \sqrt{120^2 - 4 \times 2575}}{2} = \frac{120 \pm \sqrt{14400 - 10300}}{2} = \frac{120 \pm \sqrt{4100}}{2} = \frac{120 \pm 64}{2}\)

\(\lambda_2 = 92 \text{ MPa}\)

\(\lambda_3 = 28 \text{ MPa}\)

Thus giving \(\sigma_1 = 92 \text{ MPa}, \quad \sigma_2 = 28 \text{ MPa}, \quad \sigma_3 = 20 \text{ MPa}\)

The principal directions can be found by substituting each principal stress back into \((\boldsymbol{\sigma} - \sigma_i \mathbf{I})\mathbf{v_i} = \mathbf{0}\) and solving for the eigenvector \(\mathbf{v_i}\), where \(i=1,2,3\).

Problem 2: Failure Analysis

A component made of steel (yield strength σy = 250 MPa) is subjected to the following stress state:

\[\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \begin{bmatrix} 150 & 50 & 30 \\ 50 & 100 & 20 \\ 30 & 20 & 50 \end{bmatrix} \text{ MPa}\]

Determine whether the component will fail according to:

  1. Maximum Principal Stress Theory (Rankine)
  2. Maximum Shear Stress Theory (Tresca)
  3. Von Mises Criterion
Show Solution

First, let's calculate the principal stresses by finding the eigenvalues of the stress tensor.

After solving the characteristic equation, we get:

σ₁ = 185.2 MPa, σ₂ = 85.5 MPa, σ₃ = 29.3 MPa

a) Rankine Criterion:

Failure if σ₁ ≥ σy

185.2 MPa < 250 MPa → Safe

b) Tresca Criterion:

τmax = (σ₁ - σ₃)/2 = (185.2 - 29.3)/2 = 78 MPa

Failure if τmax ≥ σy/2 = 125 MPa

78 MPa < 125 MPa → Safe

c) Von Mises Criterion:

σVM = √{½[(185.2-85.5)² + (85.5-29.3)² + (29.3-185.2)²]}

σVM = √{½[9940 + 3158 + 24336]} = √18717 = 136.8 MPa

136.8 MPa < 250 MPa → Safe
